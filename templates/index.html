Research

My PhD research focuses on the physics of relativistic jets launched by quasars — specifically, on what the radio-to-X-ray emission of their kiloparsec-scale segments can tell us about the underlying flow. Working with Łukasz Stawarz, I develop models that go beyond the common homogeneous one-zone approximation: the jet is treated as a current-carrying, axially symmetric outflow in magnetohydrostatic equilibrium, whose pressure, toroidal magnetic field, and velocity-shear profiles are linked self-consistently.

In my recent first-author work I applied this framework to ten Chandra-resolved quasar jets at z ≥ 2.5. The Bayesian evidence favors radiating electrons that trace the gas pressure rather than the magnetic energy density, and points to jet powers reaching ~1049 erg s−1 — systematically higher than one-zone estimates suggest.

On the methods side, I care about doing the statistics properly: nested sampling and Bayesian model comparison, high-performance scientific computing, and — increasingly — simulation-based inference.
